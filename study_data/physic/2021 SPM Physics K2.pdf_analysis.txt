## Q7 (Page None)

The intensity of light that strikes the surface of zinc metal does not affect the kinetic energy of electron. Explain why.


Final answer (1 line):
The kinetic energy of an emitted electron depends on the frequency of the incident photon and the work function of the metal, not the intensity of the light, because each electron absorbs only one photon.
- step 1: According to Einstein's photoelectric theory (hf = Φ + KE), the kinetic energy (KE) of a photoelectron is given by KE = hf - Φ, where 'h' is Planck's constant, 'f' is the frequency of the incident light, and 'Φ' is the work function of the metal.
- step 2: This equation shows that the kinetic energy of the photoelectron is directly dependent on the frequency (f) of the incident light and the work function (Φ) of the metal.
- step 3: The energy of a single photon is E = hf, which means it is determined by its frequency, not the intensity of the light.
- step 4: In the photoelectric effect, each electron can only absorb one photon. Therefore, the kinetic energy gained by an electron is determined by the energy of that single photon it absorbs.
- step 5: The intensity of light relates to the number of photons striking the metal surface per unit time. Increasing the intensity increases the number of photons, which leads to more electrons being emitted (increasing the photoelectric current), but it does not change the energy of individual photons, and thus does not affect the maximum kinetic energy of the individual emitted electrons.
